Halloween in Wrentham

Delayed by storm, Wrentham celebrates Halloween four nights after Oct. 31

By Travis Andersen

 |  Globe Staff  

  November 05, 2011

  • Article
  • Discuss

ROSE LINCOLN FOR THE BOSTON GLOBE

Nine-year-olds Stacie Smith (vampire) and Hailey Lennon (cat) trick-or-treated with their fathers on East Street in Wrentham, one of about two dozen communities that postponed Oct. 31 celebrations.

WRENTHAM - The comic book superhero Wolverine does not normally walk the streets here on a Friday night in early November.

But last night was anything but normal in Wrentham, where trick-or-treaters went door to door for candy after a nor’easter last weekend made the annual rite almost impossible on Oct. 31.

But 5-year-old Dermott Amorim was more than happy to don his Wolverine costume last night and hunt for candy in his neighborhood with his family. “We can double trick-or-treat!’’ he said on East Street.

His parents, Peter and Michelle, said it was a second night of trick-or-treating, since they took Dermott and his 3-year-old brother Marco out on Halloween night in nearby Easton.

But Michelle Amorim, who made Dermott’s Wolverine outfit and Marco’s Captain America suit, said she was glad to have a chance to take them out in their hometown. “Halloween is our favorite time of the year,’’ she said.

It was delayed in Wrentham and about two dozen other communities after last week’s storm dropped record amounts of autumn snow and knocked out power for hundreds of thousands for days.

Other towns and cities plan to trick-or-treat today and Sunday.

Brian O’Connor, 39, stood outside his home on East Street near the Amorims before getting ready to take his 6-year-old son, DJ, around the neighborhood in a skeleton suit complete with face paint and a cape.

O’Connor said there was one silver lining to the postponement; Halloween candy sold at a bargain rate this week.

“That was the best part, the CVS price,’’ O’Connor said. “I got about eight bags [for] half price.’’

As for having to postpone trick-or-treating for several days, O’Connor said, “The first thing you think about it is, ‘Oh, no, you can’t do that.’ But then you think about the big picture . . . There’s really no way we could have done it’’ on Halloween night.

Jennifer Vieira echoed those sentiments while trick-or-treating with her sons Daniel, 12, and Colby, 10. “It’s kind of a bummer to do it late, but we had no power,’’ Vieira said.

Still, Daniel, who dressed as a self-described “scary clown’’ with a hoodie, face paint, and multicolored wig, harbored a little disappointment over the delay last night. “It’s kind of upsetting,’’ he said. “Because we could have done it maybe just a day before’’ last night.

The trick-or-treating crowd appeared to be thin in the town at about 7 p.m., with many houses darkened.

But Linda LeBlanc and her husband, Bill, had decorated their colonial on Tilting Rock Road with a holiday staple: two enormous, beaming eyes staring out from the second story windows. LeBlanc said that her daughters, Alex, 12, and Taylor, 10, were going door-to-door last night as SpongeBob and Piglet.

She said the girls wanted to trick-or-treat on Halloween night in another town, but “I made them wait. . . . Good things come to those who wait.’’
